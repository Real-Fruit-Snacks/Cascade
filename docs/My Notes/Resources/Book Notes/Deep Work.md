# Deep Work

#books #productivity #focus

## Core Thesis

Cal Newport defines **deep work** as "professional activities performed in a state of distraction-free concentration that push your cognitive capabilities to their limit." These efforts create new value, improve your skills, and are hard to replicate. The opposite — **shallow work** — is logistical, non-cognitively demanding, and easy to replicate even when distracted.

Newport's central claim: deep work is becoming increasingly rare at exactly the same time it is becoming increasingly valuable. Those who cultivate the ability to do it will thrive; those who don't will fall behind. It is both a skill and a habit.

---

## The 4 Rules

### Rule 1 — Work Deeply

Depth must be scheduled, not hoped for. Newport outlines four philosophies:
- **Monastic**: eliminate all shallow obligations (Knuth-style)
- **Bimodal**: divide time into clearly defined deep and shallow periods (weeks or seasons)
- **Rhythmic**: build a daily deep work habit at a fixed time (most practical for most people)
- **Journalistic**: fit depth wherever you can (requires mastery, not for beginners)

For me: **Rhythmic philosophy** — 6:00–9:00 AM block, no meetings, no Slack.

### Rule 2 — Embrace Boredom

The ability to concentrate is a skill that atrophies without practice. Constant context-switching trains your brain to crave novelty. Newport recommends scheduled "productive meditation" — thinking through a hard problem while walking, running, or commuting — and deliberately resisting the urge to reach for your phone when bored.

Don't take breaks from distraction; take breaks from focus. The default should be concentration.

### Rule 3 — Quit Social Media

Not an all-or-nothing argument. Newport proposes the **craftsman approach to tool selection**: adopt a tool only if its positive impacts on your goals substantially outweigh its negative impacts. Most social media fails this test. It offers mild, diffuse benefits at the cost of a permanently fragmented attention span.

Applied a modified version: deleted Twitter/X, kept GitHub (professional necessity).

### Rule 4 — Drain the Shallows

Shallow work is inevitable but should be constrained. Tactics:
- **Schedule every minute of your workday** (time-block planning)
- **Quantify depth** by asking how long it would take to train a smart recent grad to do this task
- **Fixed-schedule productivity**: set an end time and work backwards to protect it
- Use a **shutdown ritual** to fully close the workday ("Shutdown complete")

## My Deep Work Protocol

**Morning block (6:00–9:00 AM)**
- No phone until after the block
- One clearly defined task written the night before
- Analog notes only during the block — no browser

**Weekly review (Sunday evening)**
- Audit shallow vs. deep hours using time-block journal
- Plan the coming week's deep work targets
- Log results in [[Dashboard]]

**Shutdown ritual**
- Review task list, confirm nothing urgent
- Write tomorrow's top task
- Say "Shutdown complete" aloud — sounds silly, works perfectly

Current weekly deep work hours: ~14. Target: 20. Still fighting afternoon meeting culture.
