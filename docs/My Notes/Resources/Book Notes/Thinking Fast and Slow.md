# Thinking, Fast and Slow

#books #psychology #learning

## System 1 vs System 2

Kahneman's central framework divides cognition into two modes:

| | System 1 | System 2 |
|---|---|---|
| Speed | Fast | Slow |
| Effort | Effortless | Effortful |
| Control | Automatic | Deliberate |
| Examples | Reading facial expressions, simple arithmetic | Complex reasoning, tax forms, novel decisions |

**System 1** is the brain's default — pattern-matching, associative, narrative-driven. It is fast and usually right in familiar environments. It is also the source of most cognitive biases.

**System 2** is the brain's analyst — careful, sequential, energy-intensive. The key insight is that System 2 is *lazy*. It endorses System 1's outputs with minimal scrutiny unless something explicitly triggers skepticism. We think we're reasoning carefully; usually we're rationalizing quickly.

The practical upshot: you cannot simply "think harder" to avoid biases. You need to build systems and checklists that externalize the reasoning System 2 is too tired to do in the moment. This connects directly to note-taking practices in [[How to Take Smart Notes]].

## Cognitive Biases

Kahneman covers dozens of biases. The ones that have stuck most for me:

- **Anchoring**: The first number you hear disproportionately shapes your estimate, even when you *know* it's arbitrary. Affects salary negotiations, project estimates, pricing intuitions.
- **Availability heuristic**: We judge probability by how easily examples come to mind. Plane crashes feel more probable than car crashes because they're more memorable.
- **Halo effect**: A positive impression in one domain bleeds into unrelated domains. Attractive people are judged as more competent; well-written proposals are judged as more technically sound.
- **Planning fallacy**: We systematically underestimate how long our own projects will take while accurately estimating others'. Inside view vs. outside view.
- **Loss aversion**: Losses loom roughly twice as large as equivalent gains. "Don't lose $100" motivates more strongly than "gain $100." Has enormous implications for [[Personal Finance]] decisions.
- **Regression to the mean**: Exceptional performance is partly luck; it regresses. Praising a good performance and criticizing a bad one makes it *look* like criticism works and praise doesn't — because of regression, not the feedback.
- **WYSIATI** (What You See Is All There Is): System 1 builds a coherent story from available evidence and doesn't flag what's missing. Confidence is a function of story coherence, not evidence completeness.
- **Substitution**: When asked a hard question, System 1 quietly substitutes an easier one. "Should I invest in this company?" becomes "Do I like this CEO?"

## Applications

**Decision-making**: Before committing to a plan, ask "what would an outside observer say about the reference class of projects like this one?" Force the outside view.

**Writing and [[Writing]] practice**: WYSIATI means first drafts are full of unstated assumptions. The reader only has the page. Slow down and externalize what System 1 thinks is obvious.

**Personal finance**: Loss aversion explains why I hold losing investments too long. Building a rule-based rebalancing system removes the System 1 emotional response from the equation. Notes in [[Personal Finance]].

**Note-taking**: The availability heuristic makes recently read ideas feel more important than older, perhaps more relevant ones. [[How to Take Smart Notes]] is partly a remedy — surfacing old notes breaks availability bias.
